Benjamin of Tudela

Also known as Binyamin mi-Tudela, Benjamin ben Jonah

People c. 1130 – 1173

Navarrese Jewish merchant whose Sefer ha-Massa'ot ('Book of Travels') of c. 1165–73 is the most ambitious medieval Hebrew geography of the Mediterranean, the Latin East, and the Jewish communities of every place between Tudela and Baghdad.

Benjamin was a Jewish merchant from the small Navarrese town of Tudela who, between roughly 1165 and 1173, undertook an exceptionally long journey across the Mediterranean and the central Islamic world — south through Christian Spain, across Provence, down the length of Italy, by ship through the Aegean to Constantinople, on through Cyprus to the Latin kingdom of Jerusalem, then overland through Syria, Mesopotamia, and as far as the borders of Persia, returning by sea by way of Egypt and Sicily.

His Sefer ha-Massa'ot — written in Hebrew, almost certainly in Tudela on his return — is the chief medieval Jewish source for the demography, leadership, and economic life of every Jewish community he visited along the way. It records the Jews of Marseilles, of Constantinople (where he is the first reliable witness to the size of the Karaite community), of Jerusalem (where he names two hundred Jews living in the city under Frankish rule), of Tiberias, Damascus, Aleppo, Mosul, and the great academies of Baghdad.

He was also a careful observer of the surrounding world: his descriptions of the Frankish kingdom, of the silk-weaving industry of Thebes, of the Crusader port of Acre, and of the political balance between the Latins and the Egyptian Fatimids in the years before Saladin make him one of the most useful single sources for the twelfth-century eastern Mediterranean — Jewish and Gentile alike.
