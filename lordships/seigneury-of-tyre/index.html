Seigneury of Tyre

Crusader Lordship Coat of arms

Tyre was arguably the most impregnable coastal fortress in the Latin East, functioning primarily as a royal domain for much of the twelfth century following its capture in 1124. The city’s massive fortifications, situated on a defensible peninsula, allowed it to withstand Saladin’s seemingly unstoppable advance in 1187 when virtually all other strongholds fell. It was during this existential crisis that Conrad of Montferrat arrived by sea, organized the city’s desperate defense, and effectively recreated Tyre as an independent, autonomous lordship. The city subsequently served as the provisional capital and rallying point of the kingdom until the recapture of Acre during the Third Crusade.

The seigneury owed a modest feudal service of twenty-eight knights, heavily supplemented by one hundred sergeants, highlighting its reliance on infantry and urban militias rather than vast feudal cavalry. Italian mercantile communities, particularly the Venetians, held significant autonomous quarters within the city, reflecting the kingdom’s growing dependence on maritime republics for economic survival and naval defense.

In the mid-thirteenth century, the lordship was formally conferred upon Philip of Montfort by the King of Cyprus, establishing a brief but powerful dynasty that acted with near-total sovereignty. This independence ended when the crown exercised a legal buy-back clause in 1284, reverting it to a royal domain. The city finally fell to the Mamluks in 1291.
