Crusader sites in and around Jerusalem

Jerusalem — the religious heart of the Latin Kingdom — was held by the Franks from its conquest in 1099 to Saladin's recovery of the city in 1187, and again briefly between 1229 and 1244. Inside the walls the Crusaders rebuilt the Holy Sepulchre as the Romanesque rotunda we still see today, established the headquarters of the Templars on the Temple Mount, and converted the al-Aqsa mosque into the royal palace. The Tower of David remained the kingdom's principal urban stronghold.

A ring of fortified estates and manor-castles guarded the approaches. Belmont, on a steep hill above Suba, was the great Hospitaller estate dominating the western approach. Aqua Bella (Ein Hemed) was a beautifully preserved Hospitaller infirmary and farm in a spring-fed glade. Latrun (Toron des Chevaliers) controlled the Judean foothills below Castellum Belvoir. Bethlehem, Hebron, and Lydda all had Crusader-era churches and small garrison structures. The Lordship of Jerusalem was administered as a royal domain rather than a granted fief.
